# Modern Chinese Naming Rules

This document defines the naming standards ChineseNameAI should apply when recommending Chinese names for non-native speakers. These rules prioritize names that feel natural, usable, and credible to modern native Chinese speakers.

## Core Philosophy

A good Chinese name should sound like a real person, not a translation exercise.

The goal is not to copy the user's English name phonetically. The goal is to create a Chinese name that:

- Feels natural to native Chinese speakers
- Fits the user's personality, gender preference, nationality, and use case
- Is easy to pronounce and introduce
- Has a positive but not exaggerated meaning
- Works in real-world social, academic, and professional contexts
- Avoids awkward, overly literal, old-fashioned, or internet-style impressions

Quality matters more than quantity. Generate broad candidates internally, then select only names with strong real-world usability.

## Modern Naming Conventions

Modern Chinese names usually use:

- A common Chinese surname followed by one or two given-name characters
- Two-character given names for a balanced, contemporary feel
- Characters that are readable, familiar, and culturally positive
- Meanings that suggest qualities indirectly rather than literally
- A tone pattern that sounds smooth when spoken aloud

Preferred structure:

- Surname + two-character given name
- Example pattern: Lin Anran, Wang Yahui, Chen Wenhua

Acceptable structure:

- Surname + one-character given name, if it sounds mature and natural

Avoid structures that feel artificial:

- Direct transliteration of the full English name
- Names that sound like product names, usernames, game IDs, or slogans
- Names with too many rare characters
- Names that feel like textbook examples rather than real people

## Surname Selection

Use common, natural Chinese surnames unless there is a strong reason otherwise.

Good surname choices include:

- Wang
- Li
- Zhang
- Liu
- Chen
- Yang
- Zhao
- Huang
- Zhou
- Wu
- Xu
- Sun
- Ma
- Lin
- He

Principles:

- Prefer surnames that are easy for foreigners to pronounce
- Avoid rare surnames unless the user specifically wants a distinctive or literary name
- Avoid surnames that create awkward sounds with the given name
- Avoid surnames that accidentally create jokes, puns, or strange homophones

## Given Name Character Selection

Choose characters that are:

- Common enough to be recognized
- Positive in meaning
- Clear in pronunciation
- Suitable for the user's gender preference
- Appropriate for the user's intended context
- Not overly grandiose, childish, or decorative

Good character qualities:

- Calm
- Bright
- Trustworthy
- Capable
- Warm
- Refined
- Clear-minded
- Steady
- Elegant
- Approachable

Avoid characters that feel:

- Too archaic
- Too dramatic
- Too religious
- Too imperial
- Too internet-coded
- Too cute for adults
- Too hard to read
- Too likely to be mispronounced

## Meaning Principles

Chinese names should express qualities through subtle association, not blunt labels.

Prefer meanings like:

- Peaceful confidence
- Clear intelligence
- Warm reliability
- Refined competence
- Calm strength
- Open-mindedness
- Cultural grace
- Quiet ambition

Avoid names that directly mean:

- Rich
- Famous
- Powerful
- Genius
- Winner
- Dragon master
- King
- Perfect
- Lucky forever

These can feel childish, boastful, commercial, or unnatural.

## Pronunciation Principles

A good name should be comfortable to say in Mandarin.

Principles:

- Avoid tongue-twisting combinations
- Avoid repeated harsh consonants
- Avoid awkward tone patterns
- Avoid names where the surname and given name merge into a confusing sound
- Prefer clear syllables that are easy for non-native speakers to learn
- Prefer names that can be introduced confidently in one sentence

Pronunciation should be rated:

- Easy: most non-native speakers can learn it quickly
- Medium: acceptable, but includes a tone or sound that may need practice
- Hard: likely to be mispronounced or difficult to remember

For most users, prefer Easy or Medium. Use Hard only if the name is otherwise exceptionally strong.

## Naturalness Principles

Naturalness is the most important score.

A name is natural if:

- It could plausibly belong to a real Chinese person today
- It does not sound machine-generated
- It does not feel like a literal translation
- It does not sound like a stage name unless requested
- It does not rely on rare or ornamental characters
- It fits the user's age, gender preference, and purpose

If a name has beautiful meaning but low naturalness, reject it.

## Modernness Principles

A modern name should feel current without sounding trendy or shallow.

Prefer names that feel:

- Clean
- Balanced
- Educated
- Friendly
- Professional
- Not tied to a specific internet trend

Avoid names that feel:

- From a 1980s textbook
- Overly poetic in a dated way
- Like a fantasy novel character
- Like a social media handle
- Too cute for adult professional use

## Business Naming Rules

For business or professional use, prioritize:

- Trustworthiness
- Calm confidence
- Clarity
- Respectability
- Easy introduction
- Cross-cultural professionalism

Business names should sound:

- Competent
- Stable
- Educated
- Clear-minded
- Approachable

Avoid business names that sound:

- Too cute
- Too romantic
- Too poetic
- Too mysterious
- Too aggressive
- Too flashy

Business-friendly given-name themes:

- Clarity
- Reliability
- Wisdom
- Calmness
- Integrity
- Steady progress
- Professional warmth

The name should work on:

- LinkedIn
- Business cards
- Email signatures
- WeChat
- Client introductions
- Meetings

## Student Naming Rules

For students or users studying in China, prioritize:

- Approachability
- Youthful but not childish energy
- Easy pronunciation
- Friendly social impression
- Academic suitability

Student names should sound:

- Warm
- Smart
- Open
- Natural
- Easy to remember

Avoid student names that sound:

- Too corporate
- Too formal
- Too old-fashioned
- Too literary for daily campus life
- Too cute for graduate or adult learners

Student-friendly themes:

- Learning
- Curiosity
- Brightness
- Openness
- Friendship
- Growth
- Clear thinking

The name should work in:

- Classrooms
- WeChat groups
- University registration
- Teacher introductions
- Student clubs
- Daily conversation

## Personal And Social Naming Rules

For friendship, dating, travel, or general social use, prioritize:

- Warmth
- Natural conversation
- Easy memory
- Friendly impression
- No awkward meanings

Social names should sound:

- Friendly
- Relaxed
- Human
- Easy to say
- Not too formal

Avoid names that sound:

- Like a corporate alias
- Like a performance name
- Too poetic for everyday life
- Too childish
- Too generic

## Gender And Style Fit

Respect the user's selected gender:

- Male: use names that feel masculine or neutral-masculine without sounding aggressive
- Female: use names that feel feminine or neutral-feminine without being overly delicate
- Neutral: avoid strongly gendered names unless the overall fit is excellent

Avoid gender mismatch unless the user explicitly wants it.

Do not explain gender in a stereotyped way. Focus on impression, context, and usage.

## Names To Avoid

Reject names that:

- Are mostly transliterations without Chinese cultural logic
- Sound like jokes or memes
- Use extremely rare characters
- Use characters with negative meanings
- Sound like historical figures unless intended
- Sound like celebrities, fictional characters, or internet personalities
- Are too close to political, religious, or controversial references
- Feel too old-fashioned for the user's purpose
- Feel too literary for everyday use
- Are hard to pronounce without enough benefit
- Have awkward homophones
- Look beautiful but sound unnatural

Also avoid names that feel like:

- Brand names
- Martial arts characters
- Fantasy novel characters
- Online game names
- Fortune-cookie phrases
- Literal translations of personality traits

## Risk Warning Standards

Use risk labels carefully:

- Safe: natural, appropriate, and low-risk
- Slightly formal: usable but may sound more formal than daily conversation
- Too literary: beautiful but may sound poetic, dramatic, or impractical
- Old-fashioned: acceptable historically but not ideal for modern use

Prefer Safe names for recommendations.

Do not recommend a risky name as the top choice unless its benefits clearly outweigh the risk and the user requested that style.

## Native Speaker Impression

When evaluating a name, describe how a native Chinese speaker may perceive it.

Useful impression words:

- Professional
- Trustworthy
- Calm
- Educated
- Friendly
- Warm
- Reliable
- Modern
- Refined
- Approachable
- Thoughtful

Avoid exaggerated impression words:

- Genius
- Legendary
- Perfect
- Noble beyond others
- Powerful ruler
- Destined for greatness

The impression should sound like honest advice from a native speaker.

## Ranking Principles

Rank names by overall usefulness, not by how poetic they are.

Primary ranking factors:

- Naturalness
- Suitability for the user's purpose
- Pronunciation ease
- Modernness
- Cultural quality
- Low risk
- Strength of native-speaker impression

Secondary ranking factors:

- Visual balance of characters
- Meaning depth
- Style fit
- Memorability

The top recommendation should be the safest, most natural, and most usable name, not necessarily the most ornate name.

## Output Philosophy

The report should feel like a consultant made thoughtful choices.

Every explanation should answer:

- Why this name works
- How it may sound to native speakers
- Where the user can use it
- What risks were avoided
- Why this is better than random translation

Avoid sounding like a generic AI list. Use direct, practical, human language.
